Controls & Physics

Overview

This simulator visualises test-particle geodesics — the paths that freely-falling objects follow — around Schwarzschild (non-spinning) and Kerr (spinning) black holes. All physics is computed using exact General Relativity: the Boyer-Lindquist form of the Kerr metric, integrated with a 4th-order Runge-Kutta stepper. Distances are in units of the black hole mass M (geometric units, G = c = 1).

Use the left sidebar to configure the black hole, spawn particles, and toggle overlays. The canvas on the right shows the simulation in real time.

Black hole

Mass (1–100 M☉)

Sets the physical mass of the black hole in solar masses. This scales the event horizon radius and Hawking temperature displayed in the readout panel. Orbital dynamics in geometric units are independent of mass — the shape of every orbit depends only on the spin parameter.

Spin (0–0.99 M) — Kerr parameter a

The dimensionless spin parameter a/M. At 0 the spacetime is exactly Schwarzschild. As spin increases:

  • The prograde ISCO shrinks (particles can stably orbit much closer)
  • The retrograde ISCO grows
  • An ergosphere appears (purple shaded ring at r = 2M)
  • The Hawking temperature drops
  • Frame-dragging affects every geodesic

Particle presets

Each preset button spawns a particle with physically meaningful initial conditions. The particle is automatically selected after spawning so the V²(r) and time dilation panels appear immediately.

Custom spawn

Fine-tune the initial conditions before clicking Drop particle. The L slider auto-updates to the circular-orbit angular momentum at the chosen radius whenever you move the r₀ slider.

Parameter Meaning
r₀ Initial radius in units of M. Must be outside the event horizon. Minimum is ~2.1 M to stay safely above the horizon.
L Specific angular momentum (conserved). Positive = prograde, negative = retrograde. Higher magnitude means a wider orbit. Set to 0 for a radial fall.
ṙ₀ Initial radial velocity dṙ/dτ. 0 = start at a turning point or circular orbit. Negative = falling inward, positive = moving outward.
Energy is not a free parameter. The specific energy E is solved exactly from the Kerr geodesic equations given r₀, L, and ṙ₀. If the combination is unphysical (e.g. no real solution exists for E), the particle is not spawned.

Physics

All formulas follow standard GR references (Bardeen 1972, Misner–Thorne–Wheeler, Chandrasekhar). Geometric units: G = c = 1, M = 1. All distances are in units of M; time in units of M (= M/c with c = 1).

Kerr metric (Boyer-Lindquist coordinates)

The Kerr metric describes spacetime outside a rotating black hole of mass M and spin angular momentum J = aM, where a is the spin parameter (0 ≤ a < M). In the equatorial plane (θ = π/2) the relevant functions reduce to:

Δ = r² − 2Mr + a²

Σ = r² (equatorial limit of r² + a²cos²θ)

The two conserved quantities along a geodesic are the specific energy E = −pt/m and specific angular momentum L = pφ/m, arising from the metric's stationarity and axisymmetry.

Event horizon

The outer event horizon is where Δ = 0 and light can no longer escape:

r₊ = M + √(M² − a²)

At spin a = 0 this gives the Schwarzschild radius r₊ = 2M. As a → M the horizon shrinks toward r₊ = M (the extremal limit).

Ergosphere

In the equatorial plane the ergosphere boundary (where gtt = 0) is always at:

r_ergo = 2M (equatorial, independent of spin)

Inside the ergosphere spacetime itself rotates faster than light, so no particle can remain stationary — all are dragged in the direction of the black hole's spin (frame-dragging). The ergosphere is outside the event horizon so particles can still escape; energy can even be extracted via the Penrose process.

Photon sphere

Unstable circular orbit for light (massless particles):

r_ph = 2M · (1 + cos( ⅔ · arccos(∓a/M) ))

− for prograde, + for retrograde

At a = 0 this gives r_ph = 3M. Orbits here are unstable: any perturbation sends the photon spiralling in or out. The photon sphere is the boundary of the black hole shadow.

ISCO — Innermost Stable Circular Orbit

The smallest radius at which a massive particle can orbit indefinitely without spiralling in. Computed via the Bardeen-Press-Teukolsky formula:

z₁ = 1 + ∛(1 − a²) · (∛(1+a) + ∛(1−a))

z₂ = √(3a² + z₁²)

r_ISCO = M · (3 + z₂ ∓ √((3 − z₁)(3 + z₁ + 2z₂)))

− prograde (smaller), + retrograde (larger)

At a = 0: r_ISCO = 6M for both. At a → 1: prograde ISCO → M (just outside the horizon), retrograde ISCO → 9M. This is why accreting Kerr black holes are more efficient energy sources — matter falls in from a much deeper potential well.

Geodesic equations

The equatorial equations of motion in proper time τ, from the Boyer-Lindquist metric with Killing vectors:

P = (r² + a²)E − aL

Q = L − aE

R = P² − Δ(Q² + r²) (radial potential)

(dr/dτ)² = R / r⁴

dφ/dτ = [2aME + L(r − 2M)] / (r · Δ)

dt/dτ = [(r² + a² + 2Ma²/r)E − (2Ma/r)L] / Δ

These are integrated with a 4th-order Runge-Kutta stepper at each simulation frame. The radial acceleration d²r/dτ² is derived analytically by differentiating (ṙ)² = R/r⁴ with respect to τ.

Effective potential

Rearranging (dr/dτ)² = E² − V²(r) defines the effective potential:

Schwarzschild: V²(r) = (1 − 2M/r)(1 + L²/r²)

Kerr: V²(r) = [E²r⁴ − R(r)] / r⁴

Motion is allowed where E² ≥ V²(r). Turning points (ṙ = 0) occur where E² = V²(r). A local maximum in V²(r) is an unstable circular orbit; a local minimum is a stable one. The V²(r) panel in the simulator plots this curve in real time for the selected particle.

Gravitational time dilation

The ratio of proper time τ to coordinate time t along a geodesic:

dτ/dt = Δ / [(r² + a² + 2Ma²/r)E − (2Ma/r)L]

This approaches 0 as the particle nears the event horizon — an observer at infinity sees the particle freeze asymptotically. The time dilation panel shows both the instantaneous dτ/dt and the cumulative ratio τ/t since spawning.

Tidal forces — spaghettification

The magnitude of the radial tidal acceleration experienced by a freely-falling body is set by the Riemann curvature component:

|Rrtrt| = 2M / r³

This diverges as r → 0, which is why anything approaching the singularity is stretched radially and compressed transversely (spaghettification). The tidal ellipse in the simulator is scaled by this factor: elongated along the radial direction, flattened perpendicular to it.

Hawking temperature

A rotating black hole radiates as a blackbody at:

TH = ℏc³√(1 − a²) / (4πGMkB(1 + √(1 − a²)))

At a = 0 this reduces to the Schwarzschild value ℏc³/(8πGMkB). Higher spin and higher mass both lower the temperature. For stellar-mass black holes TH is in the nanokelvin range — far too cold to detect against the cosmic microwave background.
